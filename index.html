Britain

How national stories get made — and who gets to make them.

Identity · Power · Memory · Narrative · Culture

↓ Scroll

This strand is not about Britain.
It's about how stories become nations.

Seven instruments, moving from the contemporary to the historical and back. Each one uses a different object — political language, music, newspapers, empire, myth, archives — to ask the same question.

Students who complete this strand aren't just prepared to discuss Britain. They're equipped to interrogate any national narrative. That's the real claim. Looking at the instruments, it's a defensible one.

The strand also asks where its own framework fails. Narrative analysis can overemphasise construction and underemphasise contingency — the moments when the story could have gone differently. The instruments don't resolve that tension. They hold it open.

What the instruments share
Principle 1 — Object before argument
Students encounter a specific object — a political speech, a music industry map, a newspaper archive, a museum object — before any analytical claim is made. The argument emerges from the encounter rather than arriving as instruction.
Principle 2 — The same question, seven objects
Every instrument returns to the same question from a different angle: who gets to tell the story, and what happens to everyone whose version doesn't survive? The repetition is structural, not accidental.
Principle 3 — What the framework misses
The strand argues that national stories are constructed and contested. But the framework can overemphasise narrative and underemphasise contingency — the moments when the story didn't have to go the way it did. Ask this at each instrument: what would the framework miss here?
Threads across the instruments
Who gets to tell it
Who Gets to Leave?, Whose Story Is This?, and Who Gets to Keep It? all ask the same question at different scales — political, journalistic, archival.
Built from elsewhere
British Invasion, Bassline Britain, and How British Is It? all examine the same mechanism: a culture claiming ownership of something it took from somewhere else.
Myth and time
Who Invented England? and How British Is It? both ask how ideas and objects come to feel ancient when they are recent — and who benefits from that feeling.
The invisible infrastructure
Bassline Britain and British Invasion both trace the gap between who built the culture and who got credited for it.
Recommended sequence

Open with Who Gets to Leave? — it establishes that language is contested before any historical material arrives.

Run British Invasion and Bassline Britain in sequence — both trace the same mechanism of cultural appropriation at different scales.

Place Whose Story Is This? after the music instruments — students arrive already thinking about who gets credit.

Use How British Is It? and Who Invented England? as a pair — one contemporary, one historical, both about amnesia.

End with Who Gets to Keep It? — it asks the strand's final question at the level of physical evidence. It is the hardest to argue your way out of.

Abitur essay questions — Themenfeld 3
To what extent is national identity constructed rather than inherited?
How does control of historical narrative function as a form of political power?
To what extent does Britain's cultural identity depend on erasing the origins of what it claims as its own?
Should cultural objects be returned to the countries from which they were taken? On what grounds could this claim be resisted?
Is sovereignty a coherent concept — or does its meaning always depend on who is using it?
To what extent can narrative analysis overstate the role of stories and understate the role of material conditions — economics, geography, military power — in shaping national identity?
Are some national narratives more historically accurate than others — and if so, what does that mean for the claim that all national stories are constructions?
